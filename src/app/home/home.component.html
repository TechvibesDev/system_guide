Keys not working on keyboard

Reasons
A common misconception of this keyboard issue is that it is caused by a virus as the user loses control over the keys. Most causes for keyboard failure involve dust and rust buildup and accumulating over the years. Or it could be from a liquid spill or leakage that many computer users might not even be aware of.
Steps
  1. Restart the laptop
    As a general rule, the first thing you should try when you encounter a computer problem that's serious enough to keep you from getting work done is to restart the computer â€” turn it off and then back on again. Doing that flushes out the computer's memory and restarts all your programs, drivers, and utilities, and that can be enough to restore your laptop to full operation.
  2. Clean the keyboard
    Sometimes, what at first seems like a serious hardware error is really a fairly mundane problem. If only some of your keys aren't working properly, for example, there could be dirt and debris under the keys, keeping them from working properly. To check if it's simply a matter of dirt under the keys, start with a small brush or microfiber cloth and wipe the keys, trying to extract any debris that might have gotten caught under or between the keycaps. You should also dab a cloth with rubbing alcohol and gently but thoroughly wipe away any dried liquids or stuck-on dirt. For the most thorough cleaning, blast a can of compressed air into the gaps between keys.
  3. Check your keyboard drivers and settings
    • In the Start search box, type 'Device manager' and click it when you see it appear in the search results
    • Click the arrow to the left of Keyboard to expand the section.
    • Right-click your keyboard in the device list and choose Uninstall device.

Video card faults

Reasons
"Video cards can fail for so many different reasons. Not properly installing the component in the computer can lead to video card failure, but more commonly, dust and lint are the culprits. Another thing that can cause video card failure is too much overclocking.
Steps
  1. Install the latest chipset drivers for your motherboard
    • To start, right-click on 'My Computer' and select 'Properties.
    • Go to the 'Hardware' Tab and choose 'Device Manager.
    • Now open 'IDE ATA/ATAPI controllers,' and you will see the manufacturer's name, e.g., VIA, Intel, NVidia, etc.
    • Now that you have the manufacturer's name, in this case, Intel, go to their site and download the latest motherboard drivers for the chipset
  2. Update your display drivers

    NB: Sometimes, your chipset drivers may be OK, but your video card is running on old and outdated drivers, which may cause the video card to fail. Here is how you update drivers on your computer.

    • Log into your computer as an administrator. Now click on the "Windows" icon button and the "R" button simultaneously.
    • Now type in "devmgmt. MSC," followed by the "Enter" button.
    • Now get to "Display adapters," right-click on the graphics card, and then choose "Properties.
    • Navigate to the "Driver" tab, and then click on "Update Driver.
    • Proceed and click on "Search automatically for updated driver software."
    • Now follow the onscreen prompts to update the display drivers for your video card.
  3. Physically check your computer

    NB: Sometimes, the problem may be a bad connection, dirty points, or poor seating. It requires you to check the hardware seating and connections on your computer physically.

    • Remove the video card and then clean it. This will remove all dust that may be covering the card and its connections.
    • Once you have cleaned the card, place it back in its slot and ensure it is firmly secured.
    • Remove the RAM cards and similarly clean them as you did the video card.
    • Return the RAM and ensure they are firmly in place.
    • Restart the computer and see how the display will behave when playing video or games.

PC not using a portion of RAM

Reasons
sometimes people run into an issue where Windows won't use full RAM. This can happen for a number of reasons. If your Windows has a 32-bit architecture, you will be limited in the amount of RAM that you can use. Outdated BIOS, inbuilt GPU, turn-on ram virtualization, and damaged RAM are some of the other possible contributors.

Troubleshooting steps
  1. Uncheck Maximum Memory
    • Right-click the Windows icon from the bottom left
    • Click Run and type MSConfig then click OK
    • Go to Boot options and click Advanced Options
    • Uncheck the Maximum Memory and click OK
  2. Turn off Virtual Memory
    • To turn off virtual memory, click on the Start Menu and search for Advanced System Settings
    • Open System Settings, under advanced click Settings
    • Click Advanced from the top and press Change
    • Untick Automatically Manage Paging File Size For All Drives
    • Select No Paging File then click OK
  3. Update BIOS
    • Go to the official website of your BIOS manufacturer
    • Find the Driver download page
    • Type System Information in the Start menu and open it
    • Compare the BIOS Version/Date in the System Information with the version number specified on the official website.
    • Begin the downloading.
    • Once the download is complete, then double-click on the downloaded file.
    • Upon opening the file, the system will warn you that updating the BIOS process will reboot your system. Click on the OK button.
    • Next, the system will show you another information dialog, which indicates the current version of BIOS and the version you are installing. Click on the OK button again.
    • The system will begin the BIOS updating process
    • Once the progress bar goes to 100%, youâ€™ll see a black screen followed by the following screen.
    • After the updating procedure, the system will reboot normally.
    • Once the system has successfully rebooted, you can confirm that the latest BIOS version is installed.
